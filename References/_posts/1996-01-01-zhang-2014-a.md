    abstract = {Chinese languages often have complex patterns of tone alternation caused by adjacent tones or the prosodic/morphosyntactic environment in which a tone appears, commonly referred to as tone sandhi. The primary goal of this chapter is to discuss the typological characteristics of Chinese tone patterns and outline the contributions that the studies of Chinese tones have made to phonological theory. Secondarily, the methodological issue of how to best study Chinese tone patterns in the future is also addressed. The issue of the formal representation of tone is highly contentious, and phonologists' arguments for any specific theory typically come from properties of tonal inventories and tone sandhi behaviors. The issue revolves around questions such as: (i) What is the Tone Bearing Unit (TBU)? and (ii) What are the primitive features of tone?.},
