The daunting nature of the undertaking arises from conflicting pressures to generalize beyond the stimuli encountered without generalizing too far. The computational and logical difficulties raised by these conflicting pressures have caused many researchers to conclude that language is not learnable by an unspecialized learning device 1-3. Rather, humans must be born with some number of built-in constraints for deciding when and how to generalize from the stimuli they encounter. This view of a constrained language learner has dominated the field for the past 30 years or so. However, recent advances in cognitive science are causing us to reconsider the type and degree of constraints placed on the learner. Of particular interest , and the focus of this article, are recent studies on in-fants' ability to acquire information about miniature artificial languages after very brief exposure.
